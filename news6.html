How To Protect Your Skin Inside Office

1. Screen Test
New medical research has found that the computer monitors can cause more than just vision troubles, it can lead to allergic skin conditions. How? Monitors create an electrostatic field around which absorbs floating dust in its surrounding. This can cause symptoms of skin diseases such as allergies, skin discolouration, blotches and rashes. "People with pre-existing skin conditions like rosacea [redness of cheeks] and photosensitivity are more in danger as radiation from the screen can aggravate these conditions," says Dr Deepak Vohra, senior consultant, dermatology at Fortis Group of Hospitals, Delhi Monitor radiation can result in itchy, dry, wrinkled skin and photo ageing - a skin condition normally associated with sun exposure. Plus dermatitis from unclean workplaces is yet another skin peril lurking in your office aisles. "Overexposure to white light at the workplace can worsen rosacea while too much air conditioning can aggravate eczema," says Dr Ajitha Bagai, senior consultant, dermatology at Max Hospitals, Delhi.

2. Skin Signs
A group of medical researchers in Sweden recently published a new finding in Pediatrics claiming that using a laptop for hours could lead to "toasted skin syndrome", normally on the thighs where the laptop is rested. The heat that causes the condition originates from the laptop's optical drive, the battery, or the ventilation fan.
The syndrome consists of a brownish discolouration of the skin caused by prolonged exposure to heat from the computer. The condition can lead to permanent darkening of the skin, and in rare cases, damage that leads to skin cancer. The researchers say mild-to-moderate heat between 43° C to 47° C is enough to cause burns. However, 44° C is enough to cause toasted skin syndrome. Interestingly, most of the experts ET on Sunday spoke to were not aware of this condition. However, there are laptops stands developed to address this specific issue. Besides investing in one, here's how to keep your skin happy when working inside or outside the office.

3. What to do if You Work Indoors
Maintain strict hygiene. Use a hand sanitiser frequently, wash your hands every couple of hours
Maintain a good distance from the monitor (at least 2 feet) and clean it regularly. A sanitiser helps here too
Use some heat protection between the body and the computer. Now you have specialised stands available for laptops that address this issue
Use a water-based moisturiser to prevent drying of skin
Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water. Avoid caffeinated drinks.
Use screen filters and if possible LCD (liquid crystal display) screens to reduce radiation

4. What to do if You Work Outdoors
Try to schedule work during cooler times of the day. Meaning keep the time from 12-4 pm for working indoors and your meetings before or after that. Wear cotton fabrics and cover yourself to avoid UV rays. Long sleeved work shirt and jeans are the most effective
Stay in the shade whenever possible
Use a water resistant sunscreen of at least SPF 30+ even on cloudy days. Sunscreen has to be applied 20-30 minutes before sun exposure and re-applied every 3 hours
Check your skin regularly for any unusual moles or spots. See a doctor promptly if you find anything that is changing in shape, size or colour, itching or bleeding
Moisturise your skin throughout the year
